What “On Time” Usually Means

In major cities, trains are generally very punctual compared with many other countries. For most daily trips, services arrive and depart close to schedule.

That said, high passenger volume means small disruptions can spread quickly, especially during morning and evening rush hours.

When Delays Are More Likely

  • Peak commute periods with crowding on major lines
  • Heavy rain, typhoons, or strong winds
  • Snowfall and freezing conditions in northern or inland regions
  • Signal checks, safety inspections, or incidents on connected lines

Regional Notes

In snowy regions such as Hokuriku(北陸) or Tohoku(東北), weather-related disruption can be much more common in winter. Even when services continue, speed restrictions may lengthen travel time.

You could see tall snow walls along the tracks in some areas, and trains may be delayed or canceled if snowfall exceeds certain levels.

In large metropolitan areas, disruption on one busy line can affect many other lines because of through-services and transfer chains.

Practical Tips for Passengers

  • Check rail apps before leaving, especially in bad weather.
  • Allow extra buffer time for appointments in winter or peak hours.
  • Keep an alternative route in mind when using large stations.
  • If delayed, station announcements and app alerts are usually reliable.
