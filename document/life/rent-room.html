1. Overview

Renting an apartment in Japan involves several steps and unique cultural expectations.

Many of these processes may feel unfamiliar to people from other countries.

One month rent fee is very typical in Japan, unlike 2 weeks rent fee is common in some other countries.

2. Required Documents and Initial Costs(初期費用, shoki hiyou)

When renting an apartment, tenants are usually required to prepare several documents and pay multiple upfront fees.

Common examples include:

  • Identification documents(身分証明書, mibun shoumeisho)
  • Proof of income or employment(収入証明書, shūnyū shōmeisho)
  • Emergency contact information(緊急連絡先, kinkyū renraku-saki)
  • Initial costs such as deposit, key money, agency fee, and first month's rent or few months' rent in advance

It can be over 300,000 JPY or even higher depending on how much the one month rent fee is

  • Meanwhile, share houses(シェアハウス) or guest houses(ゲストハウス) have very lower initial costs, like 30,000-50,000 JPY
  • You don't need the fridge, washing machine, or other appliances, as they are usually provided in shared accommodations.
  • So, that can be a great option to live in a share house or guest house.

3. Guarantor System

There are two main types of guarantors in Japan:

  • 保証人, hoshounin, guarantor
  • 保証会社, hoshou gaisha, guarantor company

Most apartments require a guarantor or a guarantor company.

This system exists because landlords want financial security in case the tenant cannot pay rent.

From my experience, a guarantor company is used, which charges a fee (usually around 50% of the monthly rent) to act as the guarantor for the tenant in most cases.

4. Emergency Contact (緊急連絡先, kinkyū renraku-saki)

Some landlords require an emergency contact, and in certain cases they prefer a family member.

You may see a phrase like:

「緊急連絡先は原則2親等以内の親族でなければなりません」

(“In principle, the emergency contact must be a relative within the second degree of kinship.”)

The key word is 原則 (gensoku), meaning “in principle”.

This means exceptions are sometimes possible. If you explain your situation, many landlords or management companies may allow a friend or employer instead. However, some are strict and accept only family members, so it is important to confirm this before signing the lease.

In some cases, the management company is very strict to the rules regarding emergency contacts.

Also, because apartments can be taken quickly, it is better to check multiple options and verify the emergency‑contact requirement early.

Landlords tend to trust family members more than friends or employers, as they are considered more reliable in emergencies.

In practice, you can often write a friend or employer without needing their signature or stamp, but some landlords may not accept it—so always check in advance.

Also, some landlords aren't very welcoming to foreign tenants, because they may have concerns about language barriers or cultural differences.

Some people think that foreign people are more likely to be noisy neighbors or cause some issues. In reality, some Japanese people experience these bad experiences caused by a few individuals, not all foreigners.

4. Apartment Rules(アパートのルール, apāto no rūru)

Japanese apartments often have strict rules regarding noise, shared spaces, and daily routines.

These rules are designed to maintain harmony among residents.

5. Move-in Orientation(入居時のオリエンテーション, nyūkyoji no oriëntēshon)

When tenants move in, landlords or management companies may provide an orientation to explain the apartment's rules and procedures.

Some landlords or agencies provide explanations about:

  • How to use appliances
  • How to separate garbage
  • How to contact management in emergencies
