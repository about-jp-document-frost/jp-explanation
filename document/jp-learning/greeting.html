Common Greetings and some useful phrases in Japan

Writing about the most common greetings, phrases in Japan and how they are used in real life from Japanese perspective.

1. "Ohayou Gozaimasu(おはようございます)" Is Not Only for the Morning

Although ohayou gozaimasu literally means “good morning," it is used in many situations that have nothing to do with time. People say it:

  • when arriving at work, even in the afternoon
  • when joining a group or entering a workplace
  • when starting a shift at any hour
  • when meeting coworkers for the first time that day

The meaning becomes closer to "I'm starting my day with you now" rather than a simple morning greeting.

2. "Otsukaresama Desu(お疲れ様です)" Is the Default Greeting in Many Workplaces

Otsukaresama desu is one of the most commonly used expressions in Japanese daily life, such as in workplaces, public spaces, and social gatherings, e-mails and so on.

It can mean:

  • hello
  • good job
  • I appreciate your effort
  • goodbye
  • excuse me
  • I acknowledge your presence

Because it carries a sense of respect and recognition, otsukaresama desu is the safest and most neutral greeting in professional environments.
You can also say it everywhere. This is one of most useful phrases in Japan.

In many offices, people say otsukaresama desu dozens of times a day. It replaces “hello," “good afternoon," and sometimes even “good evening."

You can also say otsukaresama to say casually. If you add desu(です) at the end, it becomes more polite and formal.

It same goes for other verbs too, and other greetings like ohayou gozaimasu(おはようございます) and ohayou(おはよう). You can still say ohayo-(おはよー), and arigato-(ありがとー), without pronouncing the "u" at the end.

3. Why These Phrases Are So Common

There are several cultural reasons why these greetings dominate Japanese communication.

A. Group Harmony (和)

Japanese workplaces value harmony and smooth relationships. Using otsukaresama desu acknowledges the other person’s effort and maintains a polite atmosphere.

B. Hierarchy and Politeness

These expressions work regardless of rank. They are safe to use with:

  • seniors
  • juniors
  • coworkers
  • clients (in some contexts)

Because they avoid direct emotional expression, they fit well in a hierarchical society.

C. High-Context Communication

Japan is a high-context culture where meaning is conveyed indirectly. Greetings that express effort, respect, and shared understanding feel more natural than simple time-based greetings.

D. Workplace Culture

Japanese work culture emphasizes:

  • long hours
  • collective responsibility
  • constant awareness of others’ workload

Otsukaresama desu functions as a way to show empathy and acknowledge the shared effort.

4. Why “Konnichiwa(こんにちは)" and “Konbanwa(こんばんは)" Are Rare in Daily Life

Although textbooks teach konnichiwa and konbanwa, these are not commonly used in workplaces or among adults.

Reasons include:

  • they sound too formal or distant
  • they do not acknowledge effort or relationship
  • they feel more like greetings for strangers
  • they do not fit the rhythm of workplace communication

As a result, otsukaresama desu becomes the default greeting in most professional settings.

5. Foreigners Often Find This Surprising

People from low-context cultures may expect greetings to be time-based, like:

  • good morning
  • good afternoon
  • good evening

But in Japan, greetings are more about relationship, effort, and social atmosphere than about time.

Understanding this helps foreigners navigate Japanese workplaces more smoothly.

6. Other Common Greetings and Expressions

Key Japanese Expressions: “Shitsurei Shimasu(失礼します)”, “Yoroshiku Onegaishimasu(よろしくお願いします)”, and “Sumimasen(すみません)”

Japanese contains several expressions that carry multiple meanings depending on the situation. Among them, shitsurei shimasu, yoroshiku onegaishimasu, and sumimasen are used extremely frequently in daily life, especially in workplaces. These phrases do not translate directly into English because they reflect Japan’s high‑context communication style and emphasis on politeness.


1. “Shitsurei Shimasu(失礼します) or "Shitsurei Shimashita(失礼しました)” — Entering, Leaving, and Phone Etiquette

"Shitsurei shimasu(失礼します)" literally means “I will be rude,” but in practice it functions as a polite formula used in several situations. "Shitsurei shimashita(失礼しました)" is the past tense form, which can be used when you have already done something that may be considered rude or when you want to express a more formal apology.

Common uses include:

  • Entering a room

Used when opening the door to an office, meeting room, or supervisor’s space. It signals respect and acknowledges that you are entering someone else’s environment.

  • Leaving a room

Said when exiting a meeting or workplace area. It softens the act of leaving and maintains politeness.

  • Starting or ending a phone call

Used as a polite opener or closer, similar to “excuse me” or “thank you for your time.”

The phrase does not imply actual rudeness. Instead, it shows awareness of social boundaries and expresses respect for the other person’s space or time.


2. "Yoroshiku Onegaishimasu(よろしくお願いします)" — A Phrase With Extremely Broad Meaning

"Yoroshiku onegaishimasu(よろしくお願いします)" is one of the most versatile expressions in Japanese. Its meaning depends entirely on context, and there is no single English equivalent. You can say "Yoroshiku(よろしく)" for a more casual tone in conversations with friends or classmates in school or other not highly formal situations.

It can mean:

  • “Nice to meet you”
  • “I look forward to working with you”
  • “Thank you in advance”
  • “Please take care of this”
  • “I appreciate your cooperation”
  • “Let’s work well together”

The phrase is used:

  • at the beginning of relationships
  • when asking someone to do something
  • when starting a project
  • when sending emails
  • when joining a team
  • when ending a request politely

Its power comes from its ambiguity. It expresses goodwill, cooperation, and social harmony without specifying details—perfectly fitting Japan’s high‑context communication style.


3. "Sumimasen(すみません) or "Suimasen(すいません)" — Apology, Gratitude, and Calling Someone

"Sumimasen(すみません)" or "Suimasen(すいません)" is another multi‑purpose expression that confuses many learners.

Form Character
Sumimasen(すみません) The more standard form. Used in both spoken and written language.
Suimasen(すいません) A casual variation often used in spoken language. Can sound more relaxed and less formal than "Sumimasen."

It can function as:

  • an apology
    “I’m sorry.”
  • a polite way to get attention
    “Excuse me.”
  • a form of gratitude
    “Thank you (for going out of your way).”

The underlying idea is acknowledging that the other person has been inconvenienced or has done something for you. This is why sumimasen can express both apology and gratitude—both involve recognizing the other person’s effort.

Examples:

  • apologizing for bumping into someone
  • calling a waiter in a restaurant
  • thanking someone who helped you unexpectedly

Because it covers so many functions, sumimasen is one of the most frequently used words in Japanese daily life.
