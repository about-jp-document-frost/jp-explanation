How to Call Someone in Japanese: Polite Expressions and Social Context

This article explains how to call someone in Japanese using polite expressions and context-aware phrases in daily life and workplaces.

1. Using Someone's Last Name + Suffix

The most common and safest way to call someone is by using their last name with an appropriate suffix.

Examples:

  • 〜さん(neutral, polite, very commonly used)
  • 〜くん (used for younger men or juniors)
  • 〜ちゃん (used for children or close friends)
  • 〜先輩(-senpai) / 〜先生(-teacher) / 〜部長(-manager) (titles)

Calling someone by their last name without a suffix is often done by your bosses and can sound rude unless the relationship is extremely close.

2: Using someone's first name

This is usually done for very close friends, family, or romantic partners. If they're not, then it can sound overly familiar or even very rude.

3. “すみません” — The Most Common Way to Call Someone

Sumimasen is used constantly to call someone’s attention.

It can mean:

  • “Excuse me”
  • “Sorry to bother you”
  • “Could I have your attention”

Typical situations:

  • calling a waiter in a restaurant
  • calling a coworker from across the room
  • calling someone you don’t know well
  • starting a conversation politely

Because it acknowledges the other person’s time and effort, it is the safest and most natural option in many situations.

4. “あのう / あの” — Softening the Approach

Ano or anou is used when you want to call someone gently or when you feel hesitant.

It expresses:

  • politeness
  • hesitation
  • respect
  • a desire not to interrupt too strongly

It is often used before asking a question or making a request.

Example:

「あのう、ちょっとよろしいでしょうか。」
“Um, may I ask you something?”

5. “ちょっといいですか” — Polite and Professional

This phrase is common in workplaces.

Meaning:

  • “Do you have a moment”
  • “May I speak with you briefly”

It is polite, neutral, and works with seniors, juniors, and coworkers.

Example:

「ちょっといいですか。」
Used when approaching someone’s desk or calling them from behind.

6. “お願いします” When Calling Someone for Help

When you need someone to do something, especially in service situations, onegaishimasu is used.

Examples:

  • calling a taxi driver
  • asking a clerk to assist
  • requesting help in an office

It expresses both a request and politeness.

7. Avoiding Direct “Hey” or “You”

Japanese rarely uses direct pronouns like:

Word Nuance / Risk
あなた sounds too direct, and not natural in most situations
あんた sounds very rude and confrontational
きみ sounds too familiar to use with strangers or superiors
おまえ sounds very rude
きさま sounds overly aggressive

These can sound rude or overly intimate.

Instead, Japanese relies on:

  • names
  • titles
  • context
  • indirect expressions

This reflects Japan’s preference for soft, non‑confrontational communication.

8. Calling Someone Without Using Their Name

In many situations, Japanese avoids names entirely.

Common patterns:

  • 「すみません」 (most universal)
  • 「ちょっと」 (informal, but common)
  • 「あのう」 (soft, hesitant)
  • 「お願いします」 (service situations)

These expressions allow you to call someone without specifying who they are, which fits Japan’s high‑context communication style.
