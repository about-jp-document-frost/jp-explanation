Why Some Japanese People May Seem Reserved Toward Foreigners

This is a personal and cultural reflection. It does not apply to everyone, but it may help explain why communication in Japan can sometimes feel distant or difficult.

At a Glance

Why Communication Can Feel Distant

Unfamiliarity

Many people have limited everyday contact with foreigners, so they may feel unsure how to begin.

Language gap

Even basic conversation can feel stressful when English is rarely needed in daily life.

Communication norms

Japanese social behavior often values calmness, restraint, and indirect expression.

Different experiences

Past impressions, self-consciousness, and cultural distance can all shape first reactions.

Summary

Some Japanese people may appear closed or cautious toward foreigners, but this is often not simple hostility. In many cases, it comes from unfamiliarity, language barriers, and a communication style that places a strong value on harmony and emotional restraint.

In Japan, people are often taught not to be too loud, too assertive, or too expressive in public. Because of that, communication styles that feel more direct or highly expressive can seem intimidating to some people. At the same time, many Japanese people do not often have deep opportunities to interact with foreigners in everyday life, so they may feel unsure about how to start a conversation.

Appearance and language can also create distance. Some people become self-conscious when they compare themselves with others, and previous negative experiences can make them even more nervous. If someone has experienced disrespect, conflict, or strong pressure before, that memory may affect future interactions.

Another important point is that people in Japan can usually live, study, and enjoy entertainment entirely in Japanese. Because English is not necessary in daily life for most people, even better school education would not automatically make everyone comfortable speaking it. This practical environment is one reason why conversations with foreigners can still feel difficult.

From my own perspective, communication style matters a lot. I feel more comfortable with people who enjoy thoughtful and respectful conversation, and less comfortable with communication that becomes too personal or teasing too quickly. In Japan, even among Japanese people, deeper discussion is not always common, partly because education has often emphasized memorization more than open discussion.

Of course, none of this means that all Japanese people are the same or that all foreigners behave the same way. There are kind, open-minded, and respectful people everywhere. Better relationships usually become possible when both sides try to understand each other’s background and communication style.

Additional Note

People in Japan who are especially comfortable with foreigners often grew up in international environments, had regular exposure to other cultures, or developed values that are less constrained by typical communication norms.
