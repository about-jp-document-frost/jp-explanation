All Subjects Are Taught in Japanese in Japan

In Japan, every subject in the school system is taught entirely in Japanese. This creates a very self-contained educational environment where foreign languages are not necessary for academic success.

At a Glance

Why the System Feels Self-Contained

All subjects in Japanese

Students can complete nearly every stage of education without using a foreign language.

Languages stay separate

English and other languages are taught as subjects, not as tools for studying other fields.

Low practical pressure

Academic success usually does not depend on using English for real learning or communication.

Long-term effect

This structure helps explain why practical English ability remains uneven for many people.

1. A Fully Japanese-Language Education System

Unlike many countries where certain subjects may be taught in English or where bilingual programs exist, Japan’s curriculum is almost completely monolingual. Students can complete their entire education—from childhood to university graduation—without using English or any other foreign language.

This includes:

  • math
  • physics
  • chemistry
  • biology
  • geography
  • history
  • economics
  • computer science (where available)
  • all humanities and social sciences

Everything is delivered in Japanese.

2. Foreign Languages Are Treated as Separate Subjects

Foreign languages such as English, German, French, Spanish, Chinese, Korean, and Russian are available in high school or university, but they are taught as isolated subjects. They are not used as mediums of instruction for other fields.

This means:

  • students study English about English
  • they do not study math in English
  • they do not study science in English
  • they do not write essays or reports in foreign languages

Foreign languages remain academic topics, not tools for learning.

3. No Need for English in Academic Life

Because all subjects are taught in Japanese, students can succeed academically without ever using English. This reduces the motivation to develop practical English skills.

Students can:

  • enter university
  • graduate
  • get a job
  • build a career

all without needing English at any stage.

4. Strong Domestic Infrastructure Reinforces This

Japan has a massive ecosystem of textbooks, reference books, online resources, entertainment, news, and academic materials, all available in Japanese. This makes it easy to live entirely within a Japanese-language environment.

5. Consequences for English Proficiency

Since English is not required for learning or daily life, many people:

  • study English for exams only
  • memorize grammar and vocabulary
  • rarely use English in real communication
  • struggle with reading technical documentation
  • rely on translation tools or AI when needed

The education system itself does not create opportunities to use English as a functional language.

6: Observations from foreign people

That is why Japanese people don't understand very basic definitions of words in academic fields, such as "What is a function?" in math, "What is a cell?" in biology, or "What is an atom?" in physics in English. They can understand those words in Japanese, but they don't understand the English words at all, even if they are very basic and common.

Most Japanese just learn English as a subject to study for exams, and they don't use English to

  • learn something
  • communicate with people from other countries
  • enjoy entertainment from other countries
  • access global information

Conclusion

Japan’s monolingual education system allows people to live, study, and work entirely in Japanese. While foreign languages are available as subjects, they are not used as mediums of instruction. This structure explains why English proficiency varies widely and why many Japanese people do not feel the need to use English in their academic or professional lives.
