Japanese IT Companies: Hiring, Training, and Work Culture

This article brings together common patterns in Japanese IT companies, including hiring backgrounds, on-the-job training, English use, and everyday technical culture.

Key Point: No need to have a formal CS degree in Japanese IT Companies

In Japan, many IT companies do not require a formal computer science degree. People often enter the industry from a wide range of academic backgrounds, including economics, chemistry, biology, and other science-related fields.

One reason is that highly specialized computer science programs have traditionally been concentrated in a limited number of universities. Because of this, companies often build teams with people who learned technical skills in different ways, including self-study, vocational training, or on-the-job experience.

Compared with some international companies, Japanese firms may place strong value on potential, communication style, university reputation, and practical experiences such as part-time work or extracurricular activities. In some cases, these factors matter as much as—or more than—grades alone.

Key Point: No need to have a great GPA

Another common feature is on-the-job training. Many companies expect employees to keep learning after they are hired, so it is possible for people without deep formal CS training to work in IT roles and grow over time.

Hiring also places value on communication style, university reputation, and practical experiences such as part-time work or extracurricular activities than focusing on GPA or formal technical credentials.

Employees English level in Japanese IT Companies

English ability varies widely across Japanese workplaces. Most of employees in Japanese IT companies recognize familiar English words used in code, UI labels, and common documentation, but that does not always mean they can read English texts, write emails, or discuss technical topics in English.

Since a large amount of software, online content, and educational material is already translated into Japanese, most of teams can work only in Japanese. When English becomes necessary, people often feel uncomfortable and rely on translation tools, AI assistants, or coworkers who are more comfortable with technical English.

In the same way, mainstream tools such as Google Chrome are widely recognized, while Firefox or Linux are often much less familiar outside technical circles. This reflects the fact that exposure to specialized tools depends heavily on a person’s role, education, and work environment.

Key Points

  • Japanese IT companies often hire from both CS and non-CS backgrounds.
  • Economics and science graduates also enter the IT industry.
  • On-the-job training plays an important role in skill development.
  • Hiring may consider personal fit and practical experience in addition to grades.
  • Many workplaces still function mostly in Japanese, even in IT roles.
  • Technical English ability and familiarity with tools like Firefox or Linux vary widely.

Note

These are broad patterns rather than universal rules. Hiring standards differ depending on the company, role, and industry.
